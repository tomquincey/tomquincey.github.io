Newton's Laws of Motion

Previous Next • What is Physics?

Summary

Newton's three laws of motion describe how any object moves when forces act upon on it, as well as giving some insight into the nature of forces themselves.

Newton's 1st Law:

An object in motion stays in motion

When no force is acting upon an object, it continues to move at the same speed, in the same direction.

Newton's 2nd Law:

\(\textbf{F}\)
\(\leftarrow\)
\(\rightarrow\)
\(\textbf{F}\)

The acceleration \(\textbf{a}\) of an object is calculated from the net force \(\textbf{F}\) acting upon it using: \[\textbf{F} = m\textbf{a}\] where \(m\) is the mass of the object.

Newton's 3rd Law:

For every action there is an equal and opposite reaction
\(\textbf{F}_{1}\)
\(\leftarrow\)
\(\rightarrow\)
\(\textbf{F}_{2}\)

\[\textbf{F}_{1} = - \textbf{F}_{2}\] For any force, there must always be a corresponding force of the same strength in the opposite direction.







WHAT!?
— Lil Jon

Movement is all around us; from people driving cars the wrong way down one-way streets, to the peaceful drifting of leaves on an autumn day, to your ice cream falling to the ground as a seagull knocks it out your hand.
Yellow-beaked munter.

The role of Newton's laws of motion is to tell us how things move, i.e. how objects respond when something called a force acts on them.

The role of all other laws of physics is to tell us why things move, i.e. they tell us what these forces are.

A very important (yet often overlooked) point to make note of is that Newton's laws, as they have so far been described in the Summary section, are completely meaningingless — for the simple reason that we have never actually described what a force is.
(You could replace all instances of the word 'force' with 'gorce' instead, and Newton's laws would be equally unhelpful.)

So what is a force? In all the time I have studied physics, the best description of force I have come across is the intuitive, yet slightly dopey notion of force simply being how hard you push or pull something.
Therefore, from Newton's 2nd law, it would follow that if you push two objects equally hard, and one was half as heavy as the other, then the lighter one would accelerate twice as much as the lighter one.

"But I thought physics was about being precise," you might point out, "yet you're describing force, one of the most fundamental ideas in physics, in a fundamentally vague way."
A very valid thing to notice. However, we are missing half of the story. We need other laws of physics to tell us what these forces are; famous examples including gravitation[-2], electrostatic attraction[-2] and Hooke's spring law. Because these laws are all precisely expressed, our vague notion of force isn't relevant.

Using Hooke's Law as an example, when you pull the end of a spring by a distance \(x\), it pulls back with a force of \(kx\) (where \(k\) is simply a constant value, called the stiffness of the spring). Move the end of the spring twice as far, the spring pulls back with twice the force.
This precise expression for the force \(\textbf{F}\), namely \[\textbf{F} = k\textbf{x}\] gives the force when the spring is moves, and then newton's laws tell us how the spring moves.

An object in motion stays in motion

Newton's first law of motion is as simple as it gets; when there is no force on an object, it continues to move at the same speed, in the same direction.

Therefore, if an object isn't moving at all (i.e. its speed \(=0 \: m/s\)), then it will continue not moving — until a force acts upon it.

You might also hear Newton's 1st Law referred to by the grandiose title "the law of inertia", but this doesn't tell you anything you didn't already know.

Despite being a scary 3-syllabled Latin-inspired word, interia is a concept you are very familiar with. It simply means the desire of something to continue doing what it's already doing.

Familiar examples include the horror or getting out of bed in the morning, the unwillingness of people these days™ to get off their phones, and the insistence of smart-arses to exclusively use the Oxford comma.

The acceleration \(\textbf{a}\) of an object is calculated from the net force \(\textbf{F}\) acting upon it using: \[\textbf{F} = m\textbf{a}\] You may have noticed that when \(\textbf{F} = 0\), then \(\textbf{a} = 0\).
No acceleration means velocity stays the same, so Newton's 1st law is actually included in the 2nd law!

This turns out to be a consequence of the Law of Conservation of Momentum, which is the most important law of all physics.
